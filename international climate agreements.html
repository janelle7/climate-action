International Climate Agreements

United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)

Adopted: 1992, at the Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro.

Purpose: To stabilize greenhouse gas (GHG) concentrations in the atmosphere and set a foundation for future climate agreements.

Key Features:

(i) A non-binding agreement with voluntary commitments from countries.

(ii) Framework for climate negotiations (Conference of Parties - COP).

Significance: Acts as the parent treaty for major agreements like the Kyoto Protocol and Paris Agreement.


Kyoto Protocol

Adopted: 1997; entered into force in 2005.

Purpose: Legally binding commitments for industrialized countries to reduce GHG emissions.

Key Features:

(i) Targets primarily developed nations (Annex I countries).

(ii) Legally binding framework with non-punitive implementation.

Significance: Universal participation, flexible commitments, and emphasis on transparency and accountability.


Montreal Protocol

Adopted: 1987; entered into force in 1989.

Purpose: To phase out the production and use of ozone-depleting substances (ODS).

Key Features:

(i) Though focused on ozone protection, it indirectly addresses climate change because many ODS are potent greenhouse gases.

(ii) Kigali Amendment (2016): Targets the phase-down of hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), a major contributor to climate change.

Significance: One of the most successful environmental agreements, demonstrating global cooperation to address environmental challenges.
